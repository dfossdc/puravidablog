## What Is Bedwetting (Nocturnal Enuresis)?

Bedwetting after age five is considered beyond normal development. It affects 10-15% of school-age children and can persist into adolescence if not addressed. Most cases are:

- **Primary enuresis:** The child has never consistently been dry at night
- **Secondary enuresis:** The child was dry for 6+ months, then started wetting again

The cause is usually not a psychiatric or behavioral problem. It is **neurological dysfunction** — specifically, poor communication between the brain and the bladder.

## The Nervous System Control of Bladder Function

Most people think bladder control is purely voluntary — "the child just needs to hold it." Actually, it is much more complex. Here is how it works:

The **bladder** receives nerve input from the **sacral plexus** (spinal nerves at L4, L5, S1, S2, S3). These nerves control:

- **Parasympathetic fibers:** Tell the bladder to contract and empty (usually during waking)
- **Sympathetic fibers:** Inhibit bladder contraction and keep the internal sphincter closed (keeping you dry)
- **Somatic fibers:** Control the external sphincter (voluntary control)

During sleep, the sympathetic (drying) signals usually dominate. The bladder stays closed. The internal sphincter stays tight. The child stays dry.

When the **sacral nerve roots (L4-S3) are compressed or misaligned**, the sympathetic signals weaken. The parasympathetic (emptying) signals get through, but the sphincters do not tighten in response. The bladder empties involuntarily. Bedwetting.

## Why Sacral Alignment Matters for Bedwetting

Most bedwetting happens because of:

**1. Sacral subluxation or misalignment.** The sacrum sits at the base of the spine, between the pelvis bones. When it tilts, rotates, or shifts, it compresses the nerve roots exiting from the sacrum. These nerve roots control bladder and sphincter function.

**2. Pelvic torsion.** The pelvis is a ring structure. When one side of the pelvis is tilted or rotated more than the other, it creates asymmetrical tension on the sacrum and alters nerve root exit angles. The sacral nerves become compressed.

**3. Dural tension.** The dura mater (the membrane surrounding the spinal cord) extends down to the sacrum. Tension in the dura anywhere in the spine can pull down on the sacral area and compress nerve roots.

**4. Lumbosacral joint dysfunction.** The joint where the lowest rib-bearing vertebra (L5) meets the sacrum can become stuck. This restricts normal motion and irritates the nerves passing through.

Any of these can impair sacral nerve function and cause bedwetting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long do I wait before seeking help for bedwetting?**
A: Bedwetting after age 5-6 can benefit from chiropractic assessment. The longer it goes untreated, the more entrenched the nervous system pattern becomes. Addressing it early helps.

**Q: Can bedwetting be caused by other things?**
A: Yes. Urinary tract infections, diabetes, sleep apnea, anxiety, and other medical conditions can cause bedwetting. I screen for these and recommend medical evaluation if needed. Chiropractic addresses the mechanical/structural component.

**Q: Should I limit fluids at night?**
A: Some families do reduce evening fluids, which can help. But fluid restriction alone does not fix the underlying nerve dysfunction. Both approaches work better together.

**Q: What about pull-ups or alarms?**
A: Pull-ups are practical for managing the immediate problem, but they do not fix the underlying dysfunction. Bedwetting alarms can help retrain the brain-bladder reflex, and chiropractic can support that retraining by improving nerve function.

**Q: My child is in their teens and still wets the bed. Is it too late?**
A: No. The nervous system remains responsive to proper alignment even in teenagers. However, the longer the pattern has been present, the longer the correction may take.

**Q: Will medication help?**
A: Some medications (like desmopressin) can reduce nighttime urine production. They manage the symptom but do not fix the underlying nerve dysfunction. Chiropractic addresses the root cause.

**Q: How often do children relapse after improving?**
A: Most children who become dry through chiropractic stay dry. Occasional relapses can happen with stress, illness, or changes in routine, but they are usually temporary and respond quickly to a visit or two.
