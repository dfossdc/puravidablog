## What Is Actually Happening in ADHD?

Most explanations of ADHD focus on dopamine and the prefrontal cortex. That is accurate at the neurochemical level. But there is a deeper layer: the **baseline state of the nervous system**.

Children with ADHD tend to have:

- **High sympathetic tone** (fight-or-flight dominance) even at rest
- **Low vagal tone** (weak parasympathetic/calm system)
- **Sleep dysregulation** (hard to fall asleep, early waking, poor sleep quality)
- **Sensory hyperawareness** (responding to stimuli that other kids filter out)
- **Emotional dysregulation** (difficulty calming down when upset)

This is not laziness or willfulness. It is a nervous system that is chronically in threat-detection mode.

When a child is stuck in sympathetic dominance, the prefrontal cortex (responsible for impulse control and focus) has less available blood flow and metabolic energy. That is why ADHD kids have trouble focusing — not because they cannot, but because their nervous system is in a state that makes sustained attention harder.

## The Cranial-Vagal Connection in ADHD

The **vagus nerve** (cranial nerve X) is the on/off switch for parasympathetic tone. It runs from the brainstem down through the neck, heart, lungs, and digestive system. A healthy vagus nerve allows the child to:

- Shift from sympathetic (alert) to parasympathetic (calm) easily
- Focus for sustained periods
- Fall asleep and stay asleep
- Regulate emotions
- Digest food and manage bowel function

When the vagus nerve is compressed or irritated by cranial misalignment, it cannot signal parasympathetic tone properly. The child stays in sympathetic dominance — hyper-focused on external threats, unable to focus on homework, unable to sleep.

## Why Cranial Alignment Matters for ADHD

The vagus nerve exits the brainstem at the occipital bone and passes through several cranial landmarks:

- **Sphenobasilar joint** controls brainstem output
- **Occipital bone** anchors the vagal exit
- **Temporal bones** affect hear and inner ear function
- **C1-C3 vertebrae** support the brainstem and vagus passage

When these structures are misaligned — from birth trauma, falls, postural strain, or developmental stress — they create mechanical compression on the vagus nerve. The result is a child locked in sympathetic dominance.

## ADHD, Sleep, and the Vagus Nerve

Sleep is critical for ADHD. When a child does not sleep well, ADHD symptoms worsen dramatically. Conversely, when sleep improves, ADHD behaviors often improve 20-30% before any other interventions take effect.

The vagus nerve controls sleep onset and sleep depth through its connection to the pineal gland and circadian rhythm centers. When vagal tone is poor, sleep is difficult. When we improve vagal tone through cranial work, sleep often improves immediately.
