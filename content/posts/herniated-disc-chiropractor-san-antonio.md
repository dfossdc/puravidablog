## Understanding Disc Herniation

Between each pair of vertebrae sits an intervertebral disc — a fibrocartilage structure with a tough outer ring (anulus fibrosus) and a gel-like inner core (nucleus pulposus). When the outer ring weakens or tears, the inner material can push through and press on nearby nerves. Common symptoms include:

- Sharp, shooting pain in the lower back, leg, arm, or neck
- Numbness or tingling radiating down an arm or leg
- Muscle weakness in the affected limb
- Pain that worsens with sitting, bending, or coughing
- Sciatica — radiating leg pain when lumbar discs are involved
- Arm pain and hand weakness when cervical discs are involved

## How Common Is Disc Herniation?

Herniated discs most commonly occur at L4-L5 and L5-S1 in the lumbar spine, and C5-C6 and C6-C7 in the cervical spine. Many people have disc herniations that are completely asymptomatic — the key is when the herniation creates nerve compression. Most cases improve significantly with conservative care within 6 to 12 weeks.

## When to Consider Surgery

While conservative chiropractic care is effective for the majority of herniated disc cases, there are situations where surgical intervention may be necessary:

- **Cauda equina syndrome** — loss of bladder or bowel control (medical emergency)
- **Progressive neurological deficit** — rapidly worsening muscle weakness
- **Failure of conservative treatment** — after 6-12 weeks of intensive care without improvement

Dr. Foss will always be straightforward with you about your prognosis. If your condition requires surgical evaluation, he will make that referral directly.
