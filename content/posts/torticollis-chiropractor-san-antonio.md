## Understanding Torticollis: What Parents Need to Know

Torticollis, from the Latin "tortus" (twisted) and "collum" (neck), is characterized by sustained contraction of the neck muscles on one side, causing the head to tilt toward one shoulder while the chin rotates toward the opposite shoulder.

**Signs and symptoms include:**

- Head consistently tilted to one side
- Chin pointed toward the opposite shoulder
- Limited head rotation, especially toward one direction
- Neck muscle tightness or visible muscle prominence on one side
- Flat spots developing on one side of the baby's head (positional plagiocephaly)
- Baby favoring feeding on one side
- Difficulty tracking objects or turning toward certain directions

**What causes infant torticollis?**

While the exact etiology varies, research points to several contributing factors:

- **Birth trauma**: Difficult or prolonged labor, forceps or vacuum delivery, breech positioning, or other birth complications can create muscle tension and spinal misalignment in the infant's delicate neck
- **In-utero positioning**: Prolonged restricted positioning in the womb can create muscular imbalances
- **Spinal subluxations** (misalignments): These can develop from the forces of birth or from in-utero positioning
- **Cranial misalignment**: Birth can compress and misalign the delicate bones of the skull, affecting nerve function
- **Muscular tightness**: Muscle shortening and tension from the above causes

The key insight: many cases of torticollis have an underlying structural component that won't self-correct with stretching alone.

## Why Conventional Medicine Often Misses the Solution

The standard medical approach to infant torticollis typically involves:

1. Reassurance that "most cases self-resolve"
2. Home stretching exercises
3. Positional therapy (encouraging the baby to turn the neck the opposite direction)
4. In some cases, referral to physical therapy
5. In severe cases, surgery (tenotomy—surgical severing of the tight muscle)

The problem: this approach addresses the *symptom* (tight muscle) without addressing the *cause* (spinal or cranial misalignment creating the neurological drive for that tightness).

Stretching a muscle that's tight because of spinal nerve irritation or vertebral misalignment is like turning off a car's check-engine light without fixing the engine problem. The light comes back on. The symptom persists.
