## What Is Actually Happening in Autistic Sensory Processing

Most explanations of autism sensory issues focus on the brain interpreting signals incorrectly. That is true at the level of neurology. But there is a mechanical component your child's brain cannot ignore: the physical structures controlling sensory gating and vagal tone.

The **vagus nerve** is the 10th cranial nerve. It runs from the brainstem down through the throat, heart, lungs, and digestive system. It carries 80% of the parasympathetic ("calming") nervous system. When vagal tone is healthy, your child can:

- Filter out irrelevant sensory input
- Stay calm during overstimulation
- Transition between activities smoothly
- Eat, digest, and sleep well
- Regulate emotions

When vagal tone is compromised — often from cranial misalignment, dural tension, or brainstem irritation — your child becomes hypervigilant, sensory-defensive, and dysregulated. Loud noises hurt. Textures are unbearable. Transitions trigger meltdowns.

This is not a behavior problem. This is a nervous system that is in fight-or-flight mode.

## The Cranial-Vagal Connection

Your child's vagus nerve exits the brainstem at the occipital bone (base of skull) and passes through several cranial landmarks:

- **The sphenobasilar joint** controls brainstem function
- **The occipital bone** anchors the vagal exit
- **The temporal bones** affect ear and vestibular function
- **The mandible** influences vagal tone through jaw tension
- **The cervical spine** (especially C1-C3) affects vagal signaling

When these structures are misaligned or restricted — from birth trauma, falls, posture, or tension — they create mechanical stress on the vagus nerve. The result is a nervous system stuck in sympathetic (fight-or-flight) dominance.

## How Cranial Chiropractic Helps Autism

My approach with autistic children focuses on restoring vagal tone and reducing mechanical stress on the nervous system:

**1. Cranial assessment.** I palpate the sphenobasilar joint, occipital bone, temporal bones, and cervical spine to identify restrictions. Most autistic children I assess have sphenobasilar dysfunction, temporal bone tightness, or upper cervical subluxation.

**2. Gentle cranial corrections.** Using SOT blocks and light mobilization, I restore proper sphenobasilar motion and brainstem function. This improves vagal tone immediately.

**3. Temporal bone work.** Releasing temporal bone restrictions helps with ear sensitivity and vestibular processing — two major sensory issues in autism.

**4. Cervical alignment.** Correcting C1-C3 misalignments reduces mechanical stress on the vagus nerve.

**5. Dural release.** Using gentle techniques, I reduce dural tension that may be pulling on the brainstem.

## What Cranial Chiropractic Cannot Do

I need to be honest about boundaries:

**It cannot "fix" autism.** Autism is a neurological difference, not a disease. The goal is not to make your child neurotypical — that is unrealistic and unnecessary. The goal is to optimize the hardware (the nervous system) so that your child can access their full potential.

**It cannot replace speech therapy, occupational therapy, or behavioral support.** These therapies teach skills. Chiropractic optimizes the nervous system so that your child can learn those skills more easily.

**It cannot address all sensory issues.** Some sensory defensiveness is purely neurological (how the brain processes input). Chiropractic helps the mechanical and vagal components, but not everything.

**It is not a substitute for medical care.** If your child has seizures, GI disorders, or other medical conditions common in autism, you need your pediatrician and specialists. Chiropractic complements this care.

## The Dural Tension Factor

One reason cranial work helps autistic children is the relief of **dural tension**. The dura mater — the membrane surrounding the brain and spinal cord — is directly attached to the sphenobasilar joint and the sacrum. Restrictions here create tension that can contribute to anxiety, hypervigilance, and sensory defensiveness.

When we restore proper alignment, we reduce dural tension and allow the nervous system to access a calmer state.

**Q: Will chiropractic make my autistic child "normal"?**

**Q: How long does it take to see changes?**
A: Most families notice calmer behavior and reduced sensory defensiveness within 2-3 weeks. Bigger changes in focus and transitions take 6-8 weeks. We reassess at 8 weeks to decide if continued care is helping.

**Q: Should I stop my child's other therapies?**

**Q: What if my child is nonverbal or cannot tolerate touch?**
A: I adapt. Some nonverbal kids prefer very light touch or no direct touch — I can work with the parent holding the child or using very gentle techniques. We make it work for your child.

**Q: Does insurance cover this?**

**Q: Can adults with autism benefit?**
A: Yes, but the nervous system is more set by adulthood. Younger children see faster, more dramatic changes.
