## What Is Spinal Stenosis?

Spinal stenosis occurs when the spaces within your spine narrow, putting pressure on the nerves that travel through the spinal column. This narrowing can develop in the cervical spine (neck), thoracic spine (mid-back), or lumbar spine (lower back). As the spaces shrink, nerves become compressed, leading to pain, numbness, tingling, and weakness—often radiating into the arms or legs depending on the location of stenosis.

The condition typically develops due to age-related changes, herniated discs, thickened ligaments, bone spurs, or a combination of these factors. While spinal stenosis is more common in people over 50, it can affect individuals of any age, particularly those with previous spinal injuries or certain genetic predispositions.

## Common Symptoms of Spinal Stenosis

People with spinal stenosis often experience:

- Pain in the lower back, neck, or both
- Numbness or tingling in the arms, hands, legs, or feet
- Weakness in the legs or arms
- Difficulty walking or balance problems
- Cramping or heaviness in the legs, especially with prolonged standing or walking
- Pain that worsens with activity and improves with rest
- Symptoms that increase when bending backward or standing upright for extended periods

The severity of symptoms can vary greatly from person to person. Some individuals experience mild discomfort, while others develop debilitating pain that interferes with daily activities and work.
