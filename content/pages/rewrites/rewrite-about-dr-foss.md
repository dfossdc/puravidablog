## SECTION 1: OPENING — THE CREDENTIAL FIRST

Dr. Dan Foss is the only Advanced SOT certified chiropractor in San Antonio. That is not a marketing claim — it is a verifiable fact. Advanced SOT certification is granted through SORSI, the Sacro Occipital Research Society International, and requires the completion of a rigorous multi-year post-doctoral curriculum covering the full Sacro Occipital Technique system, CMRT (Chiropractic Manipulative Reflex Technique) organ adjusting, and SOT Craniopathy. Worldwide, fewer than several hundred practitioners have completed the full Advanced SOT curriculum. In San Antonio, Dr. Foss is the only one.

The significance of that credential is not about a title. It is about what it permits a chiropractor to do that a general practitioner cannot. Most chiropractors treat the spine — and many do it skillfully. Advanced SOT training adds two complete clinical systems to that foundation: the ability to identify and treat the organ-spinal nerve reflexes that cause spinal dysfunction to persist despite repeated adjustment, and the ability to assess and correct the 22 bones of the skull in a rhythmic system that governs tension throughout the entire dural tube from the occiput to the sacrum. In practical terms, it means that when a patient comes to Pura Vida with a problem that has not resolved anywhere else, Dr. Foss has clinical tools that most other practitioners in this city have never trained in.

Pura Vida Chiropractic is built entirely around this three-system framework — spine, organs, and cranium treated as one integrated whole — because that is the only framework Dr. Foss has ever believed was sufficient. Not sufficient for the credential. Sufficient for the patient.

## SECTION 2: THE ORIGIN STORY — WHY CHIROPRACTIC

Dan Foss was born with torticollis. For those unfamiliar with the condition, torticollis is a persistent, involuntary twisting of the neck — a structural problem that causes the head to tilt to one side and resist correction. As a child, it was painful and limiting in the ways any chronic physical condition is limiting for a child: in movement, in comfort, in the quiet background experience of simply being in a body that does not cooperate.

The first recommendation from a medical doctor was pharmaceutical. Medication to manage the symptoms. Dan's parents considered this, and they made a different choice. They took him to a chiropractor instead. He does not remember the practitioner's name. He does remember what happened. One adjustment changed everything. The torticollis resolved in a way that no medication had touched. Dan was a child, too young to articulate what had occurred, but old enough for it to set something in motion that would define the next several decades of his life: the conviction that the body has an innate capacity to heal when the interference holding it back is removed.

That is not a philosophical abstraction. For Dan, it was a lived, physical experience before it was ever an idea. The pharmaceutical path would have managed a mechanical problem with a chemical intervention, leaving the structural cause in place. The chiropractic path found the source, corrected it, and allowed the body to do what it was already trying to do. He was too young to understand the mechanism at the time. But he understood the outcome. And that outcome became the seed of everything that followed.

More than two decades later, Dan sees the same dynamic play out with patients regularly. A person arrives at Pura Vida having spent months or years being managed — medications for the pain, medications for the symptoms the first medications caused, referrals that led to more referrals, adjustments that helped temporarily and then stopped helping. They have been offered pharmaceutical solutions to mechanical, neurological, and organ-based problems. They have not been offered an examination thorough enough to find the actual source. That is what Pura Vida is for. Dan knows what it feels like to have a structural problem treated pharmacologically. He knows because he was that child. It is why he does what he does.

## SECTION 3: THE TRAINING PATH — THE JOURNEY TO MASTERY

The path from that childhood chiropractic adjustment to Advanced SOT certification was not accidental. Looking back, it reads like a purposeful progression toward a single destination — even the parts that did not feel purposeful at the time.

Dan graduated from Western States Chiropractic College in Portland, Oregon in 2003. Western States had a strong SOT curriculum, and the technique resonated with him immediately: systematic, whole-body, category-based, and built around the idea that the body's own forces — gravity, respiration, the cranio-sacral rhythm — are partners in correction rather than obstacles to overcome. He left school not looking for the fastest path to a comfortable practice, but for the broadest possible clinical education. That instinct took him somewhere unexpected. Within months of graduation, he was practicing chiropractic in Costa Rica.

Those first years in Costa Rica were formative in ways that had little to do with technique. Practicing cross-culturally meant learning to listen differently — to read a patient when shared language was limited, to adapt communication and examination to patients who had different relationships to healthcare, different expectations, different histories. It also meant treating a wider variety of conditions than a new graduate in a conventional American practice would typically encounter. Spain followed, and with it two more years of clinical depth across a European patient population. Then Ireland. Then the Netherlands. Then Guatemala. Each location brought different pathologies, different cultural contexts, different demands on clinical flexibility. By the time Dr. Foss returned to the United States and settled in San Antonio, he had practiced chiropractic on four continents, in multiple languages, across more clinical contexts than most practitioners encounter in a full career.

San Antonio was not an accident either. The city's large Latino population — Spanish-speaking patients who often felt marginalized in English-only healthcare environments — was a community Dr. Foss was uniquely positioned to serve. He joined an established practice as an associate, deepening his roots in the city. In 2010, he founded Pura Vida Chiropractic on NW Military Highway, building the practice from the ground up around the whole-body three-pillar approach that had been taking shape in his clinical work for years. The Advanced SOT certification through SORSI — including the full CMRT and Craniopathy curricula — came through years of post-doctoral study that formalized and deepened what he had been building toward since Western States. It was not a credential he pursued for distinction. It was a curriculum he pursued because it was the most complete clinical system he had found, and he wanted to master it fully.

## SECTION 4: WHAT DR. FOSS BELIEVES — CLINICAL PHILOSOPHY

**"Symptoms are the last thing to appear and the first to go away — treating them is not the same as fixing the problem."**
By the time the body produces pain, the dysfunction driving that pain has usually been present for weeks, months, or years. Pain is the body's last signal, not its first. When we treat only the pain, we are responding to the alarm without addressing the fire.

**"The spine is connected to every organ in your body. You cannot fully treat one without understanding the other."**
The viscero-somatic and somato-visceral reflexes are not theoretical constructs — they are documented neurological pathways. Every organ shares its nerve supply with a spinal segment. Ignoring that relationship is not conservative care. It is incomplete care.

**"The skull moves. When it stops moving properly, the whole body pays the price."**
The 22 bones of the cranium articulate in a rhythmic cycle driven by cerebrospinal fluid production. The dural tube connects the occiput to the sacrum without interruption. Cranial restriction transmits tension through the entire spine. This is anatomy, not speculation.

**"An adjustment that doesn't hold is an adjustment that found the compensation — not the cause."**
When the same vertebra subluxates again and again despite repeated correction, something upstream is driving it. The answer is usually an organ reflex, a cranial restriction, or a pelvic instability that was never properly categorized. The question is never "why won't this patient hold their adjustment?" The question is always "what is pulling them out?"

**"Every patient deserves to be heard in their own language. Health has no language barrier at Pura Vida."**
This is not a policy. It is a conviction formed through years of practicing medicine cross-culturally. Therapeutic trust requires real communication. Real communication requires speaking the patient's language — and meaning it.
