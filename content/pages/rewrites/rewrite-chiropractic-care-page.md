## Pillar 1: The Spine & Pelvis — Sacro Occipital Technique (SOT)

Sacro Occipital Technique was developed in the 1920s and 1930s by Dr. Major Bertrand DeJarnette, a chiropractor and osteopath who recognized that the relationship between the sacrum at the base of the spine and the occiput at the base of the skull was the organizing principle of spinal function. When that relationship is healthy, the entire spine — and through it, the nervous system — functions as it was designed to. When it is disrupted, the consequences ripple upward and downward through every structure in between.

SOT categorizes spinal dysfunction into three distinct types — Category I, II, and III — based on specific clinical indicators rather than symptom location alone. Category I addresses pelvic distortion and foundational spinal imbalance. Category II addresses sacroiliac joint instability and disc involvement. Category III addresses cervical and extremity dysfunction in the context of an already-stabilized pelvis. The category determines the treatment sequence, ensuring that each adjustment addresses the right layer of dysfunction in the right order. Rather than using high-force thrusting techniques, SOT correction relies on specially designed pelvic blocks placed under the pelvis while the patient lies face down. Gravity becomes the corrective force, allowing the sacrum and ilium to realign gently and precisely over the course of several minutes. The result is a correction that the body's own weight reinforces — not one imposed against it.

SOT is appropriate for patients of all ages and body types, including those who have had previous spinal surgeries, pregnant patients, elderly patients, and athletes seeking performance optimization alongside injury recovery. The sacroiliac joint is the foundation of the entire spinal column. When it is stable, everything above it has a stable base to work from.

## Pillar 2: The Organs — CMRT (Chiropractic Manipulative Reflex Technique)

There is a documented neurological relationship between every major organ in the body and a specific region of the spine. The stomach shares its nerve supply with the fifth thoracic vertebra. The liver and gallbladder connect to vertebrae T6 through T9. The adrenal glands communicate through T9 to T11. The kidneys relate to T10 through L2. The reproductive organs, bladder, and bowel connect to L1 through S4. These relationships are not theoretical — they are the anatomical basis of the viscero-somatic reflex, the mechanism by which an organ under stress reflexively causes the vertebra at the other end of its nerve supply to subluxate.

This is the reason some spinal problems never fully resolve with spinal treatment alone. When a patient's mid-thoracic spine keeps subluxating in the same place despite repeated adjustments, the question that must be asked is whether an organ is driving that pattern. If the gallbladder is chronically stressed, it will keep pulling T6 through T9 out of alignment. Adjusting those vertebrae without addressing the gallbladder is a temporary intervention applied to a persistent cause. CMRT breaks that cycle. Developed through the Advanced SOT curriculum, CMRT uses Chapman's neurolymphatic reflex points — specific locations on the anterior and posterior body surface that correspond to organ function — in combination with direct organ manipulation techniques to normalize organ-spinal nerve relationships. When the viscero-somatic reflex resolves, the spinal adjustment that follows tends to hold in a way it never has before.

The somato-visceral reflex runs in the opposite direction: a chronically misaligned vertebra stresses the organ at the other end of its nerve supply. Patients with long-standing spinal dysfunction sometimes develop organ symptoms as a consequence — digestive irregularities, hormonal disruption, respiratory sensitivity — that resolve when the spinal problem is corrected. CMRT addresses both directions of this reflex simultaneously. It is available only through Advanced SOT certification, and Dr. Foss is the only certified CMRT practitioner in San Antonio.

## Pillar 3: The Cranium — SOT Craniopathy

The skull is commonly imagined as a fixed, solid structure — a helmet of bone that protects the brain but does not itself move. This is not accurate. The skull is composed of 22 individual bones joined by fibrous articulations called sutures. These sutures remain mobile throughout adult life, allowing the cranial bones to flex in a rhythmic cycle that occurs six to twelve times per minute. This rhythm is driven by the continuous production and reabsorption of cerebrospinal fluid — the fluid that bathes and nourishes the brain and spinal cord. The cranial rhythmic impulse, or CRI, can be felt by a trained clinician anywhere on the body, and its rate, amplitude, and symmetry provide precise diagnostic information about the state of the nervous system.

The dural tube — the tough membranous sleeve that contains the cerebrospinal fluid — runs continuously from the interior of the skull all the way down to the sacrum, where it attaches at S2. This anatomical reality means that the cranium and the sacrum are in direct mechanical communication with each other. Restriction in the cranial bones transmits tension down the dural tube and into the sacrum. Dysfunction at the sacrum transmits tension upward into the cranial base. SOT recognized this relationship from its earliest development, and SOT Craniopathy provides a systematic method for assessing and correcting each of the 22 cranial bones, the sutures between them, the sphenobasilar synchondrosis (the joint at the center of the skull base), and the temporal bones. There is also a specific relationship between the temporal bones and the hip joints — clinicians often find that patients with chronic low back pain and TMJ dysfunction are dealing with the same foundational imbalance expressed in two different locations.

Treatment through SOT Craniopathy is feather-light. The forces applied to the cranial bones are measured in grams, not pounds. Patients often describe the experience as profoundly relaxing — a deep release of tension they did not realize they were holding. For patients who have received spinal adjustments that never seemed to hold, or who have experienced concussion, jaw trauma, or any significant head injury, cranial evaluation is essential. The cranium is the upstream structure in the cranio-sacral system. When it is restricted, everything downstream — the dural tube, the sacrum, the entire spine — is working against a tide of tension that no amount of spinal adjustment will permanently overcome.
