## A Herniated Disc Diagnosis Does Not Have to Mean Surgery

Receiving a herniated disc diagnosis can feel frightening. The images — a disc bulging out of its space, pressing on a nerve — make it seem like surgery is the only logical answer. But for the vast majority of herniated disc patients, conservative, non-surgical care is not only viable but preferable. Research consistently shows that most disc herniations improve significantly with proper conservative care, and many resolve entirely.

## Understanding What a Herniated Disc Actually Is

The intervertebral discs are gel-filled cushions that sit between each pair of vertebrae in your spine. They serve as shock absorbers and allow the spine to flex, extend, and rotate. Each disc has a tough outer ring (the annulus fibrosus) and a soft inner core (the nucleus pulposus).

A herniation occurs when the inner material pushes through a tear or weakness in the outer ring. Depending on the direction and degree of herniation, the displaced material may press on nearby spinal nerves, causing pain, numbness, tingling, or weakness in the areas those nerves serve. Lumbar disc herniations commonly produce sciatica and lower back pain; cervical herniations can cause arm pain, hand numbness, and neck pain.

What many people do not realize is that discs do not herniate in isolation. They herniate because the mechanical environment of the spine has placed them under abnormal, prolonged stress. This is exactly what SOT targets.

## How SOT Creates the Conditions for Disc Healing

**Eliminating the Mechanical Cause:** SOT begins by analyzing pelvic and spinal mechanics to identify the pattern of imbalance that led to the disc herniation. A tilted pelvis, a rotated sacrum, or a compensatory spinal curve all place uneven compressive forces on specific disc levels. By correcting these patterns using gentle pelvic blocking and targeted adjustments, Dr. Foss removes the abnormal stress that caused the herniation and allows the disc to begin recovering.

**Decompressing the Affected Disc:** Pelvic blocking in SOT works with gravity to gently traction and decompress the lumbar spine. As the pelvis levels and the sacrum repositions, the vertebral segments above open up — creating space for the herniated disc material to retract and for the annulus to begin repairing itself.

**Reducing Nerve Irritation:** The neurological symptoms of a herniated disc — the shooting pain, the numbness, the weakness — are caused by nerve compression and the inflammatory response around the disc. As compression decreases through SOT care, nerve irritation subsides and symptoms progressively improve.

**Cervical Disc Herniations:** For herniations in the neck, SOT uses a combination of pelvic correction (since the cervical spine's position is influenced by the foundation below it), upper cervical adjustments, and cranial techniques to relieve pressure on the affected nerve roots and restore normal cervical curves.

## SOT vs. Surgery: Understanding Your Options

Surgery for disc herniation is appropriate in a defined set of circumstances — particularly when there is rapidly progressing neurological deficit such as loss of bowel or bladder control, or significant muscle weakness. However, the majority of herniated disc cases do not meet this threshold, and many patients are referred to surgery prematurely.

Conservative chiropractic care, including SOT, allows the body's natural healing capacity to work. Disc tissue has some capacity for self-repair when the mechanical environment is corrected. Post-surgical spines often develop complications, scar tissue, and adjacent segment degeneration over time — problems that do not arise from conservative care.

Dr. Foss will always provide honest guidance about what SOT can realistically accomplish for your specific disc condition and will refer appropriately if surgical evaluation is genuinely indicated.

## What to Expect During SOT Disc Care

Most disc herniation patients in our San Antonio clinic begin with a series of two to three visits per week for the first several weeks. Sessions are gentle and focused — no twisting, high-force adjustments near the affected disc level during the acute phase. As symptoms stabilize and improve, the frequency of care is reduced and the treatment progresses to longer-term structural correction.

Home recommendations typically include specific resting positions that facilitate decompression, temporary activity modifications, and gentle mobility exercises as healing progresses.
